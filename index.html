What is Violence Against Women in PNG?

Violence against women in PNG (Papua New Guinea) refers to widespread physical, sexual, emotional, and economic abuse experienced by women and girls across the country. Papua New Guinea has one of the highest reported rates of gender-based violence in the world.

Types of Violence and How To Deal With Them

🚨 Domestic Violence

What it is: Beating, intimidation, marital rape, and controlling behavior by husbands or partners. Many cases go unreported due to fear, stigma, or economic dependence.

How to Deal With It:

  • Create a safety plan (know where to go, keep important items ready).
  • Try to avoid arguments in isolated places where escape is hard.
  • Reach out to a trusted adult, family member, or community leader.
  • Contact the Family and Sexual Violence Units (FSVU) or safe houses.
  • Document incidents (dates, injuries, witness names).
  • If possible, report to police or a Family Support Centre.

⚠️ Sexual Violence

What it is: Rape and sexual assault, including gang rape (often referred to locally as "pack rape"). Violence can occur both within families and in public spaces.

How to Deal With It:

  • Get medical help as soon as possible at a clinic or hospital.
  • Avoid washing or changing clothes if you plan to report (to preserve evidence).
  • Tell a trusted adult immediately.
  • Report to the police or medical professionals who can document evidence.
  • Seek counseling or mental health support to process trauma.
  • Know that this is NOT your fault.

🔥 Sorcery Accusation–Related Violence (SARV)

What it is: Women are often accused of practicing witchcraft ("sanguma") and are tortured or killed as punishment.

How to Deal With It:

  • Seek immediate protection from authorities or community leaders.
  • Move to a safe location quickly, as this type can escalate fast.
  • Contact police, safe houses, or crisis centers immediately.
  • Reach out to organizations like FSVAC for protection and support.
  • Document threats or allegations made against you.

💍 Forced and Early Marriage

What it is: In some communities, girls are married young, limiting education and increasing vulnerability to abuse.

How to Deal With It:

  • Speak to a teacher, counselor, or trusted adult immediately.
  • There are organizations that can intervene and support your education.
  • Know that forced marriage is illegal in PNG.
  • Contact FSVAC or local education authorities for help.
  • Seek protection from safe houses or community leaders.

💼 Workplace Violence

What it is: Discrimination and harassment in professional settings.

How to Deal With It:

  • Report to a supervisor, HR department, or a trusted authority.
  • Keep records of incidents (messages, dates, witnesses, locations).
  • Document all interactions related to the harassment or violence.
  • Speak to colleagues or other affected workers to understand patterns.
  • Know your workplace rights and escalate to senior management if needed.
  • Seek legal advice if necessary.

💬 Important Reminder

None of this is your fault. You deserve to feel safe and respected. Speaking up can be hard, but it can also be the first step to protection and change.

Real Voices

Jenella
Age 39 · Central Province

"I went to the police 17 times. I went every week for the last month. They said this is a domestic problem. They just told my husband not to do it again."
Jenella married at age 14 and has 7 children. Her husband shattered her left arm, leaving it covered in scars from reconstructive surgery with metal pins. She also has scars on her head where her husband's second wife attacked her with a knife. After unsuccessfully seeking police intervention 17 times, she struggles physically and financially to care for their children. "So far I have two bones broken. It's very hard for me to find food for the kids and send them to school."

Human Rights Watch, 'Bashed Up' Report, 2015

Kere
Highlands Region

"My husband continued bashing me. He was hitting me between counseling sessions."
Like many survivors, Kere was subjected to police-mandated mediation with her abuser instead of being offered real protection. Her husband promised to stop in the presence of police officers, but continued to beat her when they returned home. The cycle of mediation without accountability is a pattern repeated across PNG.

Human Rights Watch, 'Bashed Up' Report, 2015

Tahina Booth
Port Moresby

"I was raped at age seven. That experience shaped the questions that have driven my life: why is violence normal? And what would it take to build something different — especially for girls growing up in places like mine?" Tahina is a former elite athlete who founded the Grass Skirt Project to address gender-based violence through sport-based programmes. She creates safe spaces where young people can talk about trauma. After one session in Hela province, a young man said: "We used to sit and plan fights. Now we sit and plan games."

— The Guardian, August 2025

Betty Awo
Morobe Province

"I've been through it too. When you stand up, you help others find the courage to stand too."
Betty is not a police officer or a lawyer, but a mother and a survivor. When women encounter violence, she is often the first person they call. She listens, writes affidavits, and counsels couples. She runs awareness sessions in schools and communities — unpaid and often running out of phone credit. Yet children now speak up when they see abuse, and village leaders call her when something happens.

The Guardian, August 2025

Anonymous Survivor
Central Province

"I wanted them to put him in jail for one or two years. But the police said, 'You have so many kids — you should go back and not do this again.'"
This experience reflects a systemic failure. Police across PNG routinely treat family violence as a private matter, sending women back to their abusers. Even in cases involving broken bones and attempted murder, officers often refuse to arrest perpetrators, instead mandating reconciliation.

Human Rights Watch, 2015

Maureen Mokai
Hela Province

"When they have mental health training, their minds change. They have more food. They feel that they are changing. That makes them reduce violence."
Maureen is a local councillor and community leader from Tari in conflict-riven Hela province. "Yes, we still live in violence, but with my programmes I do community backyard change."

From parliament to the grassroots, PNG's leaders and changemakers are demanding an end to violence against women. Their words carry the weight of a nation's conscience.
